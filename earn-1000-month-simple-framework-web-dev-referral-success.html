Step 1: Understand the "Why" and "What"

  • Why Businesses Need Dev: Businesses need web and software development to solve problems (e.g., outdated online presence, inefficient manual processes, lack of an online store, need for a custom app) or seize opportunities (e.g., reach new customers, automate operations, launch a new product). Understanding these underlying drivers is crucial.
  • What Offline Pixel Computers Does: Familiarize yourself with our core services:

    • E-commerce Solutions (e.g., online stores, marketplaces)

    • Mobile App Development (MVP to full-scale)
    • UI/UX Design & Consulting
    You don't need to be an expert, just know our general capabilities.

This initial understanding forms your foundation. It allows you to recognize opportunities when they arise.

Step 2: Identify Potential Leads (Active Listening & Observation)

This is where your everyday interactions become your primary tool. Think of yourself as a problem-spotter, not a salesperson.

  • Listen in Conversations: Pay attention when friends, family, former colleagues, or acquaintances who own businesses talk about their challenges. Do they mention needing a better online presence, automating tasks, or launching a new idea?
  • Observe Businesses Around You: Look at local businesses you frequent. Does their website look outdated? Is their online ordering system clunky? Do they have a manual process that could be digitized?
  • Scan Online Networks: Browse LinkedIn for businesses announcing growth, new services, or pain points. Are there social media posts from business owners seeking solutions?

The goal here is not to hard-sell, but to identify a potential need that aligns with our services.

Step 3: Qualify the Opportunity (Brief & Targeted Questions)

Before making an introduction, a quick qualification process maximizes efficiency for everyone. You want to ensure the lead is genuinely interested and has the means to proceed.

  • "What problem are you hoping to solve?" (Identifies the core need).
  • "Have you considered setting a budget for this project?" (Gauges financial readiness – a rough estimate is fine).
  • "Who would be the key decision-maker for this?" (Ensures we're talking to the right person).
  • "What's your ideal timeline for this?" (Indicates urgency).

These questions can be woven naturally into a conversation. If their answers align, you have a qualified lead.

Step 4: Make a Warm Introduction (The High-Leverage Action)

This is the simplest, yet most critical, step. Your role is merely to connect the dots.

  • Offer to Connect: "Based on what you've shared, I know an excellent web and software development team, Offline Pixel Computers, who specializes in [their specific need]. I could make a warm introduction if you'd like to explore how they could help."
  • Facilitate the Call: If they agree, simply set up a brief introductory call (e.g., 15-30 minutes) where you introduce your contact to our team. An email intro works too.
  • Let Us Handle the Rest: Once the introduction is made, our experienced team takes over. We conduct the detailed consultations, prepare proposals, and manage the entire development process. You don't need to be involved in the technical or sales follow-up.
