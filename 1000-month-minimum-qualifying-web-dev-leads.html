The Blueprint for Qualifying Web Dev Leads for High Commissions:

Before you make that warm introduction to Offline Pixel Computers, gather some key information. Think of these as your qualification questions:

1. The Need: What is the Specific Problem or Goal? (Identify a clear pain)

  • "What digital challenges is your business currently facing?"
  • "What specific outcome are you hoping to achieve with a new website/app/software?" (e.g., "increase online sales by X%", "streamline internal operations," "launch a new product line").
  • Avoid vague answers like "just need a website." A qualified lead knows *why* they need it.

2. The Budget: Is There Financial Capability? (Avoid time-wasters)

  • "Do you have a general budget range in mind for this project?"
  • "Have you allocated resources for this digital investment?"
  • You don't need exact figures, but understanding if they have a realistic budget (e.g., "mid-four figures," "five figures") is crucial. This directly correlates to your potential commission.

3. The Decision-Maker: Who Has the Authority? (Talk to the right person)

  • "Who will be the primary decision-maker for this project?"
  • "Will anyone else need to approve the project?"
  • Connecting us directly with the person who can say "yes" (or at least influence the "yes") dramatically speeds up the process.

4. The Timeline: Is There Urgency? (Prioritize motivated clients)

  • "What is your ideal timeline for launching/completing this project?"
  • "Is there a specific deadline or event driving this need?"
  • Clients with a clear timeline are more likely to be serious and ready to engage.

5. The Existing Solution (or lack thereof): What's their current state?

  • "What are you currently using to handle [their need/problem]?"
  • "What are the pain points with your current solution/process?"
  • Understanding their existing setup (or lack of one) provides context for the scale of the required new solution.

How to Qualify Without Being Intrusive:

You don't need to grill your contacts. These questions can be woven naturally into a conversation or a brief initial email exchange. Frame them as you trying to understand their needs better to make the best introduction possible.

Example: "Based on what you've told me about your website being slow, it sounds like you're looking for something that can handle more traffic and give a better user experience. Do you have a rough idea of the timeline you're looking at for this upgrade, or a general investment range you've considered? That helps my development partner prepare best for the intro call."
